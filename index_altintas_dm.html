What is the Integrated Stress Response?

The Integrated Stress Response (ISR) is an evolutionarily conserved, essential pathway that governs translational reprogramming in response to diverse environmental challenges, including nutrient deprivation, oxidative stress, hypoxia, and proteotoxic insults. By phosphorylating the eukaryotic translation initiation factor eIF2α, the ISR suppresses global cap-dependent translation while promoting selective synthesis of stress-adaptive proteins. Transcripts of these proteins contain stress-responsive elements (i.e., uORFs) within their 5’ untranslated regions (5’UTRs).

Central question

Originally characterised as a protective and transient mechanism, ISR persists in chronic pathological contexts, including cancer, neurodegeneration, metabolic syndromes, and ageing. However, its causal role in disease progression was so far unknown.

Key discoveries

  • Two functional uORFs in the MET oncogene 5′UTR mediate stress-inducible MET translation.
  • Genetic/pharmacologic ISR perturbations reduce stress-induced MET accumulation, invasive growth, and render cells more sensitive to chemotherapy.
  • Upregulation of the MET oncogene is a functional requirement linking integrated stress response to cancer progression. For more information: Cerqua, M. et al. The EMBO Journal (2025)
